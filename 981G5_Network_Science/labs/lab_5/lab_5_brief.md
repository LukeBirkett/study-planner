## Question 3: About ERGMs (a bit advanced)
In a previous lab class, we looked at generating networks with low or high assortativity but (a) we didn't specify a target value and (b) we didn't control for other properties. That is something we can do by using ERGMs. So, in this exercise, you are going to download some dataset (pick whatever you like but avoid something too big or too small) and then decide 2 or 3 properties you might want to preserve, e.g., average degree, a global clustering coefficient and degree assortativity.

Now, as discussed in the lecture notes, the probability of a network $G$ is given by:

$$P(G) \propto exp(\theta^T \mathbf{x}(G))$$

where $\mathbf{x}(G)$ are network statistics (e.g., number of edges, triangles) and $\theta$ are the parameters associated with those quantities (which control how much each feature contributes). The issue with this formulation is that the denominator (called the partition function) that guarantees those probabilities to sum up to 1 is intractable in most cases. We therefore need some ‘trick’ to approximate what a draw from the distribution would look like. That is a MCMC sampler.

The principle is as follows:
1. Initialize an initial candidate $g_{current}$. Here, the simplest one is a sample from the ER model. If the number of edges is one of your quantities of interest, you could set the probability $p$ accordingly.
2. Rewire the candidate by adding or removing one of its edges. This gives you two networks: $g_{current}$ and $g_{proposed}$.
3. Decide whether to keep the rewiring or not, according to the acceptance ratio ($AR$), defined as:

$$AR = \frac{Pr(\mathbf{G} = g_{proposed})}{Pr(\mathbf{G} = g_{current})} \cdot \frac{q(g_{current} | g_{proposed})}{q(g_{proposed} | g_{current})}$$

where $q(a|b)$ is the probability of proposing network $a$ when the current network is $b$. In the simplest case, $q(a|b) = q(b|a)$, which is the case when we toggle whether an edge exists or not. This is called a symmetric proposal. We accept the change with probability $min(1, AR)$. The intuition here is that if $AR > 1$, then $g_{proposed}$ is more likely to appear in the distribution and is therefore immediately selected. Otherwise, we randomly pick a candidate with probability equal to ratio. Now, the calculation of $AR$ can be simplified by noting that:

$$\log(AR) = \log \left( \frac{Pr(\mathbf{G} = g_{proposed})}{Pr(\mathbf{G} = g_{current})} \right) = \log \left( \frac{exp(\theta^T \mathbf{x}(g_{proposed})) / \kappa}{exp(\theta^T \mathbf{x}(G_{current})) / \kappa} \right) = \theta^T \delta_{\mathbf{x}(g)_{i,j}}$$

where $\delta_{\mathbf{x}(g)_{i,j}} = \mathbf{x}(g_{proposed}) - \mathbf{x}(g_{current})$, i.e., the difference in the counts of each quantities of interest (number of edges, triangles).

4. Steps 1-3 create a Markov chain over possible samples from the ERGM distribution. Repeat them for $n$ steps, and eventually the chain will converge to an appropriate sample from the ERGM distribution.

Generate a number of samples and compare their network statistics with those of the original network.
