While much of the prominent literature by Elva Robinson, Nigel Franks, and their colleagues focuses on the "house-hunting" paradigm (where a colony's nest is destroyed or a migration is forced), tandem running is a general-purpose recruitment and information-sharing mechanism. When a colony is safe at home, the primary trigger for tandem running switches from finding a new home to foraging for food.

In small-colony ants like the rock ants (Temnothorax albipennis, Temnothorax nylanderi, and Temnothorax rugatulus)—the very same genus studied in the Robinson et al. (2011) paper—tandem running serves as their primary way to recruit nestmates to rich, static food resources.

Research on Temnothorax rugatulus has shown that when the colony is securely nested, the triggering of tandem runs is highly dependent on food quality. Scouts that discover a highly concentrated sucrose solution will return to the safe nest and immediately perform a tandem run to lead a single follower to the food

In house-hunting, when a colony reaches a critical "quorum" threshold, they stop tandem running and switch to social carrying (physically picking up nestmates and carrying them to the new nest) because carrying is three times faster. However, carried ants learn nothing about the route. When a colony is safely settled at home and merely foraging, they continue to rely on tandem running because the primary goal is teaching. By guiding a follower step-by-step, the leader allows the follower to learn the visual landmarks along the path. Once the follower knows the route, she can return to the safe nest and become a leader herself, establishing a steady pipeline of foragers.

By "forward tandem" (or a forward tandem run), the authors mean a tandem run where a knowledgeable leader guides a naive follower from the old/unsafe nest forward to the new candidate nest. The addition of the word "forward" specifies the directional flow of information and ants during the early stages of a colony migration.

"reverse" tandem run.

Forward Tandem Runs: These occur in the early phase of exploration and evaluation. An ant finds a new site, returns to the old nest, and leads a follower forward to the new site to show them the way and have them evaluate it.

Reverse Tandem Runs: These occur later in the emigration process, typically after the colony has reached its "quorum threshold" and full-scale social carrying (physically carrying nestmates) has begun. In a reverse tandem run, a recruiter leads a follower backwards from the new nest site to the old nest site.


While a forward tandem run is clearly designed to recruit new decision-makers to evaluate a potential home, the function of a reverse tandem run has been heavily debated in the literature (including by Franks and colleagues).  Biologists have found that reverse tandem runs do not contribute to the decision-making process of choosing a nest. Instead, they are believed to serve a few alternative engineering/organizational purposes:
1. Recruiting More Help: Bringing ants that are stranded or uncommitted at the new nest back to the old site to assist with the heavy lifting of carrying the remaining brood and passive ants.
2. Activating "Lost" Scouts: Guiding ants that might be poor navigators or lost back to the resource pile (the old nest) so they can enter the active workforce.
3. Re-coalescing Splits: If the colony accidentally splits between two new nests, reverse tandem runs are thought to help pull ants back to a single focal point.
